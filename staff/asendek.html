Biography

"Science does not aim at establishing immutable truths and eternal dogmas; its aim is to approach the truth by successive approximations, without claiming that at any stage, final and complete accuracy has been achieved."

         - Bertrand Russell, Sceptical Essays



Austin Sendek is a Ph.D. student in the Department of Applied Physics. Austin's research interests focus on the development and deployment of new computational approaches, grounded in concepts from machine learning (ML) and artificial intelligence, to accelerate the design of materials for energy storage applications. By leveraging previously reported experimental data on material performance going back over the last several decades, Austin builds models that predict and identify promising new materials with demonstrably higher efficiency and efficacy than human intuition. He then confirms these model predictions in-house through density functional theory calculations and through experimental collaborations with the Cui group. Early data suggests his model for predicting superionic character in solids offers up to several-fold acceleration in research efficiency over trial-and-error experimentation.

His contributions to the scientific world have been highlighted in the Stanford Report, ABC7 San Francisco, Scientific Computing magazine, the Los Angeles Times, and The Economist. A summary of his most recent paper can be found here. His research is funded by a seed grant from the TomKat Center for Sustainable Energy.

Austin is an active member of the Stanford energy and environmental student community: he is a former Co-President and Vice President of the Stanford Energy Club, Stanford's largest student-run energy organization; an alumnus of the Woods Institute for the Environment's Rising Environmental Leaders Program; has co-authored a scientific paper on a next-generation battery material with former U.S. Secretary of Energy and Nobel laureate Steven Chu; and was the founding co-director of the Stanford Energy Storage Community.

Austin holds a B.S. in Applied Physics from the University of California, Davis, graduating with highest honors in 2012. As an undergraduate, he worked with Prof. Daniel Cox to build computational models of dying brain cells in Alzheimer's disease and predicted a new mechanism for the characteristic cell collapse often observed experimentally. Austin grew up in the rural Northern California town of Yreka, where he developed his love for skiing, fishing, and backpacking. In 2010, Austin made headlines for his whimsical quest to establish the Northern California slang term "hella" as the SI prefix for one octillion; this quest continues today from Stanford.
