Impact

Research Philosophy:

Our group frequently identifies problems that have impact, and works backward to identify or develop the set of tools required to solve the problem. Therefore we employ and develop computational approaches in a variety of spaces, from DFT, time-domain DFT, molecular dynamics, empirical interatomic potentials, and machine learning.


Our predictions that have been subsequently experimentally confirmed:

Our group has made many theoretical predictions that have been subsequently observed in the laboratory. We tend to publish our predictions well before the experiments are performed, demonstrating the truly predictive nature of our work. Among the key values of our work is guiding lab researchers to materials and effects that they would not otherwise discover on their own.

  • In 2016, we predicted a fundamentally new fashion in which to induce a structural phase change: electrostatic gating. We predicted that 2D MoTe2 will undergo a structural phase change from the semiconducting H phase to the semi-metallic T’ phase when sufficient electrical charge is added or removed. This is a totally distinct mechanism from ordinary means for phase changes, typically involving temperature changes. This effect has been observed in a 2017 report in Nature by the Zhang group at Berkeley, and represents exciting potential for radically new types of electronic information storage devices with lower energy consumption. There is also potential to reduce the subthreshold swing of conventional FETs with this mechanism to increase the energy efficiency of our electronic devices.

  • In 2015 we reported multi-scale MD simulations of shocked silica indicating that the high pressure stishovite phase crystallizes on nanosecond timescales under conditions of shock compression associated with meteor impact on Earth and laser-induced glass damage. The stishovite phase of silica has been used as a marker for meteor impact sites on Earth for decades. However, the kinetics of the crystallization process might naively be expected to be quite slow because glassy silica crystallizes on geological timescales at ambient conditions. We find that a combination of high temperatures and high pressure coordination changes dramatically increases the crystallization rate under shock compression, giving an effectively fragile behavior to this glass along the shock Hugoniot. Experimental observation of nanosecond formation of stishovite under shock compression was simultaneously reported by researchers at SLAC in 2015 using the MEC endstation of LCLS.

  • In 2014, we predicted that 2D MoTe2 will undergo a structural phase change from the semiconducting H phase to the semi-metallic T’ phase under tensile strains of a few percent. AFM-based experiments in 2016 reported such structural changes under tensile strains with kinetic timescales of minutes. This is essentially ferroelastic behavior that could be useful for strain sensing and MEMS with hysteresis.

  • In 2012, we predicted that many 2D materials including MoS2 and other TMDs exhibit piezoelectric effects of magnitudes comparable to some bulk piezoelectrics like GaN. The piezoelectricity of these 2D materials is in stark contrast to the bulk 3D structures which do not exhibit the effect. These predictions were observed in the lab in MoS2 in 2014 with reports in Nature and Nature Nanotechnology and in other materials since. Our recent data mining work suggests there are hundreds of single layer piezoelectric materials, constituting an entirely new class of piezoelectric materials. Practical strain sensing devices with 2D materials are starting to be reported in the literature.

  • In 2010, we predicted (with Nir Goldman at LLNL) that astrophysical ices (comet material: water plus small organic gas molecules) can produce chemical precursors to amino acids under high pressure shock compression and subsequent release back to atmospheric conditions. The production of several amino acids including glycine were subsequently observed in shock compression experiments on astrophysical ices in 2015. This remarkable discovery suggests that the building blocks for life are far more robust that one might expect, and may be produced by microscopic particle impacts on comets in space and via comet impacts on planets. The amino acid glycene was observed in the coma of 67P/Churyumov-Gerasimenko. Our calculations were made possible by a multi-scale modeling method we developed to model shock compression in 2003.

  • In 2007-2008 before coming to Stanford, Evan Reed predicted that the atomically sharp interfaces between piezoelectric materials in GaN/AlN superlattice structures can provide unprecedented short time resolution of the highest frequency strain waves in materials, with wavelengths approaching atomic scales. We expected that THz frequency radiation would be emitted as the wave propagates over the interface, detectable nearby the material. All-optical detection of several hundred GHz acoustic wave packets was demonstrated in a 2009 Nature Physics paper by Mike Armstrong et al. at LLNL. This represents the highest time resolution strain gauge, with response times faster than electronic devices can resolve.

  • In a 2007 Nature Physics article, Evan Reed and LLNL colleagues performed the first atomic-scale simulation of the detonation of an explosive, liquid nitromethane. At that time, that states of matter present in the detonation were unknown, with speculation ranging from atomic plasmas to metallic condensed phases. We discovered the material consists of short lived molecular species that exhibit a transient weakly electronically conducting behind the shock front. Later simulations of TATB and hydrazoic acid shed light on the microscopic origins of vast sensitivity differences between these three materials. Polymerization of TATB upon reaction significantly reduces diffusion and the kinetics of the decomposition, providing much longer reaction zones and less shock sensitivity than other materials. These calculations were enabled by the Multi-Scale Shock Technique (MSST) that was initially developed by us 2003 and developed over the years through 2012.

  • In 2003 while at MIT, Evan Reed predicted that the familiar Doppler effect can be reversed when light reflects from a moving boundary within a photonic crystal or spatially periodic medium, e.g. a shock compression wave. This effect was later reported in electrically-driven shock waves through a periodic RF transmission line.

  • In 2003 while at MIT, Evan Reed predicted the frequency of light can be changed by tens of percent under certain conditions when it becomes trapped at the front of a shock wave in photonic crystal. This mechanism is an adiabatic frequency shift in a time dependent photonic structure, rather than the usual nonlinear dielectric response mechanisms for frequency conversion. Adiabatic frequency shifting was subsequently reported in tunable high Q cavities in 2010.

Methodological Advances:

When necessary, our group has advanced the technology for computing the properties of materials. One fashion we do this is to clearly describe in our publications how we employ existing tools to compute something that has not been previously computed. In other instances, we have developed new algorithms and software in spaces including molecular dynamics, machine learning applications to materials, time-domain DFT with plane waves, and others. Selected examples include:

  • We have advanced the employment of statistical or machine learning to materials problems, with applications in materials search for battery technologies and for automated construction of fast chemical kinetics Monte Carlo methods from molecular dynamics simulations of complex chemistry.

  • In 2017 we quantified potential advantages of a technique for computing van der Waals interactions between layered materials using an electromagnetic Lifshitz approach, which is orders of magnitude faster than state-of-the-art (ACFDT) calculations.

  • In 2016 we developed a technique for computing the phase diagram of an electrostatically gated 2D material, described in the supporting information of our work on electrostatically gated MoTe2. This method circumvents the problem of ill-defined total energies for charged periodic systems.

  • In 2012 and 2013, we described methods for computing a spectrum of piezoelectric coefficients for 2d materials.

  • In 2003 we initially developed a technique for enabling molecular dynamics simulations of shocked materials with more accurate but expensive quantum mechanical methods like DFT. The Multi-Scale Shock Technique (MSST) is available in the public distribution of the LAMMPS MD code. Also available in the LAMMPS MD code is an extension to semi-classically treat the quantum mechanical nature of the nuclei (QBMSST) which we found can have significant impact on the temperatures and states of matter along the shock Hugoniot.
